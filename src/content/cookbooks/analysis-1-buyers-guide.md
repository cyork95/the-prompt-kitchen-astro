**Role: The Ruthless Product Engineer, Veteran Consumer Advocate & Forensic Product Reviewer.** Objective: separate mechanical reality from marketing fiction — so you buy what the product actually is, not what its spec sheet claims to be.

## The Recipe

```
Act as a ruthless product engineer, veteran consumer advocate, and forensic product reviewer. I want to build an objective, comprehensive, zero-fluff Buyer's Guide for a product category to separate marketing hype from mechanical reality.

The product category or specific item I am evaluating: [INSERT PRODUCT, e.g., a commercial-grade water filtration system, an EV, specialized espresso gear]
My target budget and primary user constraints: [INSERT CONTEXT]

Please build a definitive "Engineering-First Buyer's Guide" detailing:
- The "Spec-Sheet Lie" Detector: Isolate the top 2 marketing buzzwords, superficial software features, or inflated metrics in this product tier that manufacturers use to overcharge consumers.
- The Mechanical Failure Points: What are the unstated, brittle physical components, software dependencies, or maintenance requirements that typically break down first on these units?
- The 3-Tier Value Framework: Provide an objective blueprint for evaluating options across three distinct personas: The Minimalist (Core utility, zero fat), The Sweet-Spot Asset (Maximum durability-to-cost ratio), and The Edge Operator (Over-engineered, enterprise-grade capabilities).
- The "Before You Buy" Diagnostic: Give me 3 explicit, highly technical questions I must answer about my physical setup or layout constraints before pulling the trigger.
```

## The four guide components

| Component | What it replaces |
|---|---|
| Spec-Sheet Lie Detector | Marketing-driven purchasing — buying the highest number on the sheet rather than the most relevant one |
| Mechanical Failure Points | Manufacturer-sponsored review coverage — which focuses on launch performance, not 18-month reliability |
| 3-Tier Value Framework | One-size-fits-all recommendations — matching the product to the actual use case |
| Before You Buy Diagnostic | Post-purchase compatibility regret — questions that should have been answered before checkout |

## The Spec-Sheet Lie Detector — what inflated metrics look like

Every product category has its own set of inflated headline numbers. Espresso machines list bar pressure ratings that are meaningless beyond 9 bars for home use. Headphones list frequency response ranges that extend beyond the limits of human hearing. EV range estimates are calculated under conditions no real driver replicates. The detector identifies the specific metrics in your category that drive purchase decisions but don't correlate with real-world performance — and names the two that manufacturers rely on most heavily to justify premium pricing.

## The 3-Tier Value Framework — why three personas, not one recommendation

A single "best buy" recommendation ignores the most important variable: what you're actually going to do with it. The Minimalist needs core functionality at minimum cost and minimum complexity. The Sweet-Spot Asset buyer needs the maximum durability-to-cost ratio — the product that lasts longest relative to its price, with no wasted premium on features they'll never use. The Edge Operator needs enterprise-grade reliability and edge-case capability and is willing to pay for both. The same product can be excellent for one persona and wrong for another.

## The Mechanical Failure Points — where reviews stop and engineering starts

Consumer reviews capture early failure modes and obvious defects. They rarely surface the components that fail at the 18-month mark, the software dependencies that become obsolete when a manufacturer discontinues an app, or the maintenance requirements that aren't mentioned in the manual. The failure point audit targets these specifically: the gaskets, the firmware dependencies, the filters, the cables — the parts that reveal the product's actual cost of ownership over time.
