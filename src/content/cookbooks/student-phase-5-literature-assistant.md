**Dense journal articles are written to survive peer review, not to be understood quickly.** This recipe cuts through the methodology sections and limitations footnotes to extract exactly what's useful for your own argument — and how to cite it accurately.

## The Recipe

```
Act as a senior research fellow and literature review expert. I am parsing dense academic literature for a research project and need to rapidly extract the core methodology, data limitations, and conceptual utility of a paper.

Here is the abstract or text of the research paper:
[PASTE RESEARCH TEXT/ABSTRACT HERE]

Please synthesize this paper into a crisp, high-utility "Research Blueprint" containing:
- The Core Methodology: What exactly did the researchers do? Detail the dataset, sample sizes, variables, and framework used in plain English.
- The Critical Gaps/Limitations: What are the explicitly stated or hidden limitations of this study (e.g., selection bias, geographic constraints, outdated data thresholds)?
- The Synthesis Bridge: Give me 3 explicit ways I can reference or leverage this paper's findings to support, counter, or provide context to a broader academic discussion.
```

## What the Research Blueprint gives you

| Section | What it tells you |
|---|---|
| Core Methodology | What they actually measured — not what the title implies |
| Critical Gaps/Limitations | Where the paper's authority ends — crucial for not overclaiming |
| Synthesis Bridge | Three ready-to-use hooks for your own argument |

## The limitations section is the most valuable part

Most students skip limitations. Graders notice. Citing a paper while acknowledging its constraints ("while X et al. found Y, their study was limited to Z population") demonstrates the kind of critical engagement that separates strong academic writing from a source dump.
