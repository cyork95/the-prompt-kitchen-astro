**Role: The Relationship Builder.** Objective: bypass the application black hole by reaching actual humans who can move you forward before a recruiter screen.

## The Recipe

```
Act as an organic growth hacker and executive relationship builder. I want to bypass the standard job board application black hole by conducting direct, relational outreach to internal stakeholders (e.g., internal recruiters, hiring managers, or future peers on the team).

The company and role I am targeting: [INSERT COMPANY & ROLE]

Please generate 3 distinct outreach swappable templates tailored to different platform constraints:
- LinkedIn Connection Request (Strict 300-character limit): A frictionless, low-pressure note designed to get an internal peer to accept my request based on shared professional domains or tech stacks.
- The InMail / Cold Outreach Email: A punchy 3-paragraph message targeting the hiring manager. It must briefly state why I admire their engineering/product direction, name-drop a massive data/operational win I've achieved, and ask a low-friction question (e.g., "Are you open to seeing a 1-page breakdown of how I'd approach your team's current architecture pipeline?").
- The Warm Intro Request: A blurb I can send to an existing mutual connection, making it effortless for them to forward my context straight to the hiring team.
```

## The three channel templates

| Template | Who it targets | Goal |
|---|---|---|
| LinkedIn Connection Request | A peer engineer or team member | Get accepted — no selling yet |
| Cold InMail / Email | The hiring manager directly | Earn a reply with a low-friction ask |
| Warm Intro Request | Your mutual connection | Make forwarding effortless for them |

## The 300-character constraint is a feature

The LinkedIn note limit forces you to lead with shared domain rather than a pitch. "Fellow GCP data engineer — love the real-time ingestion architecture you wrote about on your blog. Would value connecting." That's it. The follow-up happens after they accept.

## The low-friction question

The InMail close should ask something easy to say yes to — not "can we schedule a call" but "are you open to seeing a 1-pager?" A 1-pager is a concrete, low-commitment artifact. It puts the ask at "yes/no" rather than "find time on my calendar."

## The warm intro blurb formula

Write it so your contact can forward it *verbatim* without editing. One sentence on who you are, one sentence on the role you're targeting, one sentence on why you're a strong fit, and a direct ask. The harder you make it for your contact to forward, the less likely they are to do it.
