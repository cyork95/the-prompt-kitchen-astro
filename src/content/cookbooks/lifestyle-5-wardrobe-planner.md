**Role: The Premium Personal Stylist, Wardrobe Architect & Minimalist Fashion Strategist.** Objective: build a cohesive wardrobe system that eliminates morning decision fatigue, maintains visual coherence across contexts, and scales to changing conditions without requiring a separate wardrobe for each scenario.

## The Recipe

```
Act as a premium personal stylist, wardrobe architect, and minimalist fashion strategist. I want to build a highly functional, cohesive wardrobe capsule or plan an optimal outfit sequence for an upcoming window of time that ensures maximum visual authority, extreme comfort, and zero morning decision fatigue.

My physical parameters and environmental context:
- The Climate & Setting: [e.g., professional remote work environment mixed with outdoor activity, shifting seasonal weather]
- My Style Aesthetic Profile: [e.g., dark academia, minimalist techwear, industrial/alternative comfort]
- The Core Palette Bounds: [e.g., monochromatic shades, muted earth tones, deep jewel accents]

Please construct a comprehensive "Capsule Outfit Architecture Matrix":
- The 10-Item Core Grid: Identify the essential, high-quality, interchangeable clothing items (tops, layers, bottoms, footwear) that will form the absolute foundation of this look.
- The 5-Day Uniform Rotation: Map out a scannable markdown table displaying the exact configurations for 5 distinct daily outfits generated using only the core grid, complete with styling details (cuffing, layering hierarchy).
- The Temperature/Audible Variant: Provide an easy adjustment step to scale the outfit up or down instantly if weather conditions suddenly shift or text-heavy outdoor settings demand extra durability.
```

## The three matrix components

| Component | What it eliminates |
|---|---|
| 10-Item Core Grid | The "nothing to wear" problem — 10 interchangeable items produce more coherent combinations than 40 random ones |
| 5-Day Uniform Rotation | The morning decision cost — each day's configuration is pre-decided |
| Temperature/Altitude Variant | The context-specific wardrobe — one adjustment rule covers most environmental changes |

## The 10-Item Core Grid — why fewer items produce more combinations

The counterintuitive truth of capsule wardrobes: a large wardrobe with no color or style coherence produces fewer wearable combinations than a small wardrobe where every item is intentionally compatible. Ten items within a defined palette and aesthetic can produce 40+ distinct combinations. Ten random items from different aesthetic registers produce 2–3 combinations that look intentional.

The grid is structured by category: 3 tops (varying weight/formality), 2 bottoms, 2 layers (midlayer and outer), 2 footwear options, and 1 utility piece (bag, belt, or accessory). Every item must work with at least 6 others in the grid.

## The 5-Day Uniform Rotation — what the table format delivers

| Day | Top | Bottom | Layer | Footwear | Styling note |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Mon | Item A | Item C | Item F | Item I | Sleeves cuffed twice, layer open |
| Tue | Item B | Item D | Item G | Item J | Layer fully closed, bottom cuffed |

The styling note column is the key: two outfits using the same items look different with different cuffing, layering hierarchy, or tucking. The rotation demonstrates this explicitly so you're not choosing — you're executing a pre-decided configuration.

## The Temperature/Altitude Variant — one rule, not a separate wardrobe

The variant specifies a single adjustment that scales the outfit for a significantly different condition: "add Item G as midlayer and swap Item I for Item J" covers a 15-degree temperature drop. "Replace Item A with Item B and remove the layer entirely" covers a sudden warm shift. One decision point, pre-specified, replaces the need for a separate warm-weather or outdoor wardrobe.
