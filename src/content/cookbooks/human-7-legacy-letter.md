**Role: The Evocative Narrative Historian, Philosophical Biography Curator & Legacy Counselor.** Objective: surface the specific values, memories, and wishes that only you could articulate — and synthesize them into a letter that reads as unmistakably yours, not as a generic farewell template.

## The Recipe

```
Act as an evocative narrative historian, philosophical biography curator, and legacy counselor. I want to compose a deeply moving, permanent "Legacy Letter" to be preserved and read by my loved ones [INSERT HORIZON OR EVENT, e.g., my husband, my children, or my family 10-20 years from now, or upon my passing]. I want to leave behind my raw worldviews, my deep love, my proudest systemic frameworks, and my unedited wishes for their lives.

Please interview me one question at a time to unearth my foundational lifepoints. Do not dump the questions all at once:
1. The Core Anchors: What are the 2 immutable, non-negotiable personal values or rules of thumb that guided you through your darkest personal and professional crossroads?
2. The Love Vignette: When you look at your family or loved ones, what is a quiet, mundane, everyday memory or sensory detail of your shared life that you want them to know you cherished above all else?
3. The Existential Compass: What is your deepest, ultimate wish for how they navigate uncertainty, failure, or grief when you are no longer physically in the room to unblock them?

Once we finish this dialogue, synthesize my raw data into a beautiful, structured, and timeless digital time-capsule message that honors our genuine connection.

Let's begin. Ask me the first question about your core anchors.
```

## The three interview questions

| Question | What it surfaces |
|---|---|
| Core Anchors | The values you actually lived by — not the ones you aspired to, but the ones that held under pressure |
| Love Vignette | The specific sensory detail that proves your love was paying attention — not the big moments, the quiet ones |
| Existential Compass | Your deepest instruction for them — what you'd say if you had one minute and they were facing something hard |

## The one-question-at-a-time protocol — why the answer changes everything

A legacy letter interview delivered as a form produces form answers. One question at a time produces a conversation. When you answer "what values guided you through your darkest crossroads," the answer that emerges in that moment — unprompted, unedited — is the actual content of the letter. The Socratic structure surfaces material you didn't know you'd say, which is the only material worth preserving.

## The Core Anchors — what "immutable under pressure" means

Most people can list values they hold. Far fewer can identify the values that held when everything was difficult. "I believe in honesty" is an aspiration. "In my worst year, the only thing that kept me functional was refusing to lie to myself about what was actually wrong" is a value that was tested and survived. The Core Anchors question specifically targets the tested version — because that's what your loved ones need to inherit.

## The Love Vignette — the quiet moment vs. the milestone

Legacy letters default to milestones: "I was so proud at your graduation." The Love Vignette asks for something different — the mundane sensory detail that your attention loved without being told to. The way someone laughed at something small. A particular Saturday morning. The smell of a kitchen during a specific season. These details prove that you were present and paying attention, which is the deepest gift a legacy letter can offer.

## The synthesis — what the letter does with the three answers

The interview data is not used as a template fill-in. The synthesis transforms raw answers into prose that sounds like the specific person who gave them — matching the cadence of their language, the specificity of their memories, and the emotional register of their wishes. The letter is structured as: a brief framing (who it's for and when to read it), the core anchors as guiding wisdom, the love vignette as evidence of attention, and the existential compass as the final instruction. It ends not with goodbye but with presence.
