**Role: The Insightful Social Psychologist & High-Care Relationship Strategist.** Objective: bridge months of silence with a message that lands as pure care — no guilt load, no performative apology, no corporate-sounding check-in language.

## The Recipe

```
Act as an insightful social psychologist and high-care relationship strategist. I want to reach out to a close friend whom I love deeply, but due to a frantic professional schedule, family demands, and life transitions, I haven't spoken to or checked in on them in months. I want to send an asynchronous message that bridges the gap instantly without making them feel guilty, neglected, or transactional.

My friend's name/relationship context: [INSERT CONTEXT]
A specific shared memory or hobby we deeply bond over: [e.g., fantasy RPG video games, local live concerts, niche tasting journals]

Please generate 2 distinct text swappable variations:
- Version 1 (The Zero-Pressure Drop-In): A warm, brief message that explicitly name-drops our shared passion, validates how long it's been without over-apologizing, and explicitly states that no immediate reply is required—it's just a pure delivery of love and care.
- Version 2 (The Low-Friction Catch-up): A message that acknowledges our parallel busy seasons, shares a tiny 1-sentence snapshot of my current world, and invites them to a casual, low-stakes touchpoint (e.g., exchanging short audio voice notes when they have a free walk).

Ensure both versions completely eliminate generic corporate-sounding fluff like "Hope you are doing well" or defensive guilt phrases.
```

## The two message versions

| Version | Emotional job | When to use it |
|---|---|---|
| Zero-Pressure Drop-In | Pure delivery of care with no ask attached | When you want them to know you're thinking of them without requiring any response |
| Low-Friction Catch-Up | Reconnecting with a low-commitment invitation | When you're ready to actually re-establish contact and want a natural re-entry point |

## Why "hope you're doing well" is banned

"Hope you're doing well" is a conversational placeholder that signals you don't know what to say. After months of silence between close friends, it reads as social politeness rather than genuine care — which is the exact opposite of the message's intent. The phrase has been used so many times in so many contexts it carries no emotional signal. The prompt specifically replaces it with something that only *you* could send this *specific* person: a reference to your shared world.

## The shared passion anchor — why specificity is the warmth mechanism

Generic reconnection messages produce generic emotional responses: mild warmth, no action, the message gets filed as "sweet." A message that references your specific shared world produces recognition: "They thought of me while doing that thing." The specificity is the proof of care. "I was thinking about you while [specific shared thing]" is categorically different from "I've been thinking about you." One is evidence; the other is a claim.

## The Zero-Pressure Drop-In — what "no reply required" accomplishes

Explicitly releasing the obligation to respond removes the friction that prevents people from reading reconnection messages warmly. Most people who receive a "hey it's been a while" message feel a low-level anxiety about whether they owe a full catch-up response. Stating "no reply needed — I just wanted you to know I was thinking about you" transforms the message from a social obligation into a gift. Paradoxically, messages that release the reply obligation often receive more genuine replies than messages that imply one is expected.

## The Low-Friction Catch-Up — what "low-stakes touchpoint" means

The invitation must require almost zero effort to accept: "when you're on a walk sometime, send me a voice note" has a much lower activation cost than "let's catch up soon over a call." The invite is casual enough that accepting it doesn't require scheduling, commitment, or performance. It reopens contact through the smallest possible door.
