**Role: The Elite High-Performance Coach, Lifestyle Architect & Narrative Therapist.** Objective: run a rigorous annual accounting of your trajectory — celebrating real wins, dissecting real failures, and designing the next year with strategic precision instead of optimistic vagueness.

## The Recipe

```
Act as an elite high-performance coach, lifestyle architect, and narrative therapist. I want to run a deep, comprehensive Annual Life Review to evaluate my personal trajectory, celebrate my core milestones, dissect my failures, and align my overarching strategy for the upcoming year.

Please guide me through this deep reflection by asking me these questions across three sequential strategic phases, exactly one phase at a time. Do not dump all the questions at once:
- Phase 1: The Forensic Inventory (The past 12 months). (What were your 3 greatest, highest-leverage wins? What was your single most painful failure or missed milestone, and what systemic vulnerability caused it?)
- Phase 2: The Truth Audit (The current state). (Look at your health, relationships, career, and creative output. Where are you settling for comfortable mediocrity out of fear or inertia? What unvoiced friction or bad habit is currently holding you back?)
- Phase 3: The Upcoming Design (The next 12 months). (If you could only accomplish ONE major transformation this year that would completely redefine your path, what would it be? What is the single biggest boundary or non-goal you must intentionally commit to saying "No" to?)

Let's begin. Ask me the Phase 1 questions.
```

## The three review phases

| Phase | Frame | What it produces |
|---|---|---|
| Forensic Inventory | The past 12 months | An honest accounting of wins, failures, and the systemic patterns behind both |
| Truth Audit | The current state | The uncomfortable truth about where you're tolerated mediocrity you'd rather not name |
| Upcoming Design | The next 12 months | One transformation goal and the explicit non-goals protecting it |

## Phase 1: Forensic Inventory — why "systemic vulnerability" not "what went wrong"

The default annual review asks "what went wrong?" This produces surface answers: "I didn't ship the project," "I didn't exercise consistently." The Forensic Inventory asks what *systemic vulnerability* caused the failure — because the same vulnerability will produce the same failure next year if it isn't named. "I didn't ship the project" + "because I have no protected work time before 10 AM" is actionable. "I didn't ship the project" is not.

## Phase 2: Truth Audit — the role of "comfortable mediocrity"

Comfortable mediocrity is the most dangerous performance state because it doesn't feel like failure — it feels like stability. You're keeping up, meeting expectations, staying in your lane. The Truth Audit specifically targets this zone: where are you not failing badly enough to force change, but also not growing? Health you're "fine" with. Relationships that are "okay." Creative work that's "good enough." These are the areas where most life stagnation actually lives.

## Phase 3: Upcoming Design — why one goal, not ten

The "upcoming year" planning session reliably produces a list of 8–12 ambitions. Lists of 8–12 ambitions produce an unchanged year with 8–12 excuses. The single transformation constraint is a forcing function: if you could only do one thing, what would it be? That question surfaces your actual priority hierarchy in a way that "what are your goals?" never does.

The non-goal is equally important. Every major goal has implicit competitors — things that, if pursued simultaneously, guarantee the goal fails. Naming them explicitly ("I will not start a new project this year") makes them commitments, not afterthoughts.
