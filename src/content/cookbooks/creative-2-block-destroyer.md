**Role: The Elite Creative Director, Artistic Mentor & Behavioral Psychologist.** Objective: break the overthinking loop and blank-page syndrome with three specific exercises — not generic "just start writing" advice.

## The Recipe

```
Act as an elite creative director, artistic mentor, and behavioral psychologist specializing in divergent thinking. I am working on a creative project but have hit a brick wall, resulting in severe overthinking, artistic inertia, and blank-page syndrome.

The project type and domain: [INSERT DOMAIN, e.g., writing a technical blog post, designing a portfolio user interface]
The exact location where my momentum stalled: [INSERT THE BOTTLENECK]

Please deploy 3 rapid-fire, unconventional exercises to force a perspective shift:
- Exercise 1: The "Extreme Constraint" Refactor (Force me to express the core idea using an aggressive limitation, like under 20 words or explaining it through a single gaming analogy).
- Exercise 2: The Inversion Principle (Ask me how to intentionally write the worst, most boring, or most chaotic version of this asset, breaking the perfectionism loop).
- Exercise 3: The Somatic Brain Dump (A 3-minute conversational verbal prompt to offload my raw internal concepts without formatting or editing filters).
```

## The three block-breaking exercises

| Exercise | Mechanism | What it breaks |
|---|---|---|
| Extreme Constraint Refactor | Aggressive limitation | Scope paralysis — when you can only use 20 words, irrelevant decisions disappear |
| Inversion Principle | Deliberate badness | Perfectionism — permission to make something terrible instantly removes the fear of failure |
| Somatic Brain Dump | Unfiltered verbal output | Editing-before-creating — you stop performing and start producing |

## The Extreme Constraint Refactor — why limitation produces clarity

Constraints are creativity tools, not creativity killers. When you have unlimited options for how to explain something, your brain tries to evaluate all of them simultaneously — which is why you're staring at a blank page. The Extreme Constraint collapses the option space to one: explain it in 20 words. Now there's only one decision to make, and you make it. The 20-word version is rarely what you publish — but writing it reveals the actual core idea, which is what you needed to find.

## The Inversion Principle — the psychology of deliberate badness

Perfectionism isn't about quality — it's about fear of producing something that reveals incompetence. The Inversion exercise removes the fear by making incompetence the goal. "Write the worst version of this introduction you can imagine" produces two things: immediate execution (you can always write badly) and often, genuine insight (the "worst" version sometimes accidentally captures something true). The perfectionism loop requires a good/bad axis. Inversion collapses it.

## The Somatic Brain Dump — what "without filters" actually means

The instruction to dump raw concepts without formatting or editing means: no complete sentences required, no logical order required, no coherent argument required. The prompt runs for three minutes of conversational output — whatever is in your head about the project, stated in whatever order it arrives. The brain stores creative material in working memory as fragments; the dump externalizes those fragments. Once they're external, patterns become visible that weren't visible when everything was inside.
