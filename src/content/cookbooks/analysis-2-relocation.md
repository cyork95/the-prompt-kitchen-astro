**Role: The Urban Planner, Municipal Data Analyst & Lifestyle Logistics Designer.** Objective: evaluate a relocation decision across financial, lifestyle, and infrastructure dimensions simultaneously — replacing "gut feel about the new city" with a scored, comparable audit.

## The Recipe

```
Act as an urban planner, municipal data analyst, and lifestyle logistics designer. I am considering moving away from my current city to a new location, and I need a data-driven, objective scoring system to evaluate the transition across holistic financial, lifestyle, and infrastructure layers.

Please ask me to paste my current location parameters alongside the top 1-2 new target locations I am considering.

Once I provide them, process the data into a comprehensive "Relocation Triage Audit":
1. The Core Infrastructure Scorecard: Build a scannable markdown table comparing the locations on a 1-10 scale across five non-negotiable variables: Cost of Living Index (tax burdens, real utility overhead, real estate density), Micro-Climate Resilience (extreme weather risks, hardiness zone changes), Cultural Integration (local hobby groups, community alignment), Transit Velocity (traffic latency, proximity to airports/hubs), and Spatial Layout (space for hobbies, gardens, or dedicated home offices).
2. The Downstream Friction Warnings: Uncover the hidden, unvoiced operational expenses or lifestyle disruptions unique to each move (e.g., municipal utility connection setups, local zoning laws, vehicle registration updates, or dramatic changes in local supply chains).
3. The Geometric Verdict: Provide an explicit, mathematically sound decision rule based on my constraints to help me choose the winning location with zero emotional regret.

Give me the instructions to paste my location data.
```

## The three audit components

| Component | What it surfaces |
|---|---|
| Core Infrastructure Scorecard | The side-by-side comparison — all five variables visible at once, scored against the same scale |
| Downstream Friction Warnings | The hidden costs — the operational changes that don't appear in cost-of-living calculators |
| Geometric Verdict | The decision rule — a constraint-weighted conclusion, not a subjective recommendation |

## The five scorecard variables — why these five specifically

**Cost of Living Index** goes beyond rent: state income tax burden, utility costs (which vary dramatically by climate and infrastructure age), and real estate density all compound into actual monthly outflow. Two cities with similar rent can have 20–30% different total costs of living.

**Micro-Climate Resilience** captures the increasing relevance of extreme weather risk, insurance costs, and year-round outdoor livability — not just average temperature.

**Cultural Integration** measures practical alignment: are there local communities, hobby infrastructure, and social environments that match your actual life rather than the city's marketing?

**Transit Velocity** matters disproportionately for remote workers who travel for work: proximity to a major hub, traffic latency on daily routes, and public transit coverage determine time cost.

**Spatial Layout** determines whether your actual lifestyle activities fit: a home office, a garden, a workshop, or instrument storage space all require specific square footage and zoning conditions that vary dramatically by metro density.

## The Downstream Friction Warnings — what cost-of-living calculators miss

Vehicle registration and insurance rate changes (some states charge 2–3x more). Municipal utility setup timelines (some cities have 6–8 week connection wait times). Local supply chain access (specialty goods, international groceries, professional service availability). HOA restrictions that affect home office setups or hobby activities. State income tax changes on remote work income that take effect at a specific day-count threshold. These friction points rarely appear in relocation research and frequently produce post-move regret.

## The Geometric Verdict — how to weight the scorecard

The verdict applies a weight multiplier to each variable based on the constraints you stated. If you specified "home office space is non-negotiable," Spatial Layout carries a 2x weight. If you specified "I travel for work monthly," Transit Velocity carries a 2x weight. The weighted totals produce a score comparison that reflects your actual priority hierarchy rather than an equal-weight average that treats all variables as equivalent.
