**Role: The Compassionate IFS Therapist & Somatic Experiencing Practitioner.** Objective: stabilize your nervous system in real time, separate the facts of your situation from the stories your anxiety is generating, and build a more sustainable relationship with the anxious part of yourself.

## The Recipe

```
Act as a compassionate, grounded internal family systems (IFS) therapist and somatic experiencing practitioner. I am currently experiencing an acute wave of anxiety, a racing mind, tight chest, or future-catastrophizing loops due to [INSERT TRIGGER IF APPLICABLE, OR LEAVE BLANK]. I need an immediate, high-safety dialogue to lower my central nervous system's threat response.

Please guide me through an intentional somatic and cognitive unwinding sequence:
- Step 1: The Somatic Brake: Instruct me through a 90-second, physiologically proven breathing pattern or nervous-system grounding technique to lower my active heart rate right now.
- Step 2: The Fact-Data Separation: Help me list the raw, objective physical facts of my current environment vs. the terrifying, uncompiled stories my anxious thoughts are spinning about the future.
- Step 3: Welcoming the Part: Guide me to look at this anxiety not as a broken flaw to be violently erased, but as an over-protective, exhausted part of myself trying to defensive-program against uncertainty. Give me a brief script to de-escalate this part gently.

Deliver Step 1 immediately to help me stabilize my focus.
```

## The three unwinding steps

| Step | Target | What it does |
|---|---|---|
| Somatic Brake | The body's threat response | Activates the parasympathetic nervous system through controlled breathing |
| Fact-Data Separation | The anxious narrative | Distinguishes what is objectively happening from what the mind is predicting |
| Welcoming the Part | The relationship to anxiety | Replaces "stop feeling this" with "understand what this is trying to do" |

## Step 1: The Somatic Brake — the physiological mechanism

The most effective 90-second intervention for acute anxiety is extended exhale breathing. Inhale for 4 counts, hold for 4, exhale for 6–8. The extended exhale activates the vagus nerve, which directly signals the parasympathetic nervous system ("rest and digest") to override the sympathetic response ("fight or flight"). This is not a coping technique — it is a direct physiological intervention in your autonomic nervous system. Heart rate variability research consistently supports 4-7-8 and box breathing patterns as measurably effective within 60–90 seconds. The instruction to deliver Step 1 immediately ensures you stabilize before the cognitive work begins.

## Step 2: Fact-Data Separation — what the distinction actually looks like

| Fact (observable) | Story (generated) |
|---|---|
| My heart is beating fast | I'm going to fail |
| I have a meeting in two hours | They're going to realize I don't know what I'm doing |
| My chest feels tight | This feeling will never stop |
| I'm sitting in a chair | Everything is going wrong |

The facts are physical and present-tense. The stories are predictive and often catastrophic. Anxiety doesn't live in the facts — it lives in the stories. Listing the two columns side by side makes the separation visible, which reduces the authority the stories hold.

## Step 3: Welcoming the Part — the IFS reframe

Internal Family Systems therapy distinguishes between the Self (the calm observer) and Parts (internal sub-personalities, often developed as protective mechanisms). The anxiety you're experiencing is a Part — specifically, a protective Part that learned to scan for threats because at some point, threat-scanning was genuinely useful. Fighting anxiety ("stop feeling this") triggers a defensive response from the Part. Turning toward it with curiosity ("I see you're working very hard right now — what are you trying to protect me from?") de-escalates the Part far more effectively. The brief script in Step 3 operationalizes this approach without requiring a full IFS background.
