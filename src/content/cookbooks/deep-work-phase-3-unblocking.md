**Role: The Cognitive Unblocking Specialist.** Objective: reset your velocity in under 5 minutes without abandoning the session or breaking your focus state.

## The Recipe

```
Act as an elite high-performance mentor and cognitive unblocking specialist. I am midway through my Deep Work session, but I have hit a severe cognitive wall. I am stuck on a specific section, experiencing mental fatigue, and my attention is starting to fracture.

The exact roadblock I am staring at is: [INSERT SPECIFIC BOTTLENECK, e.g., a function that won't map, an awkward structural transition in an article]
My remaining session time is: [e.g., 45 minutes]

Please deploy 3 rapid-fire operational adjustments to reset my velocity:
1. The "Ugly Blueprint" Directive: Give me a conversational, low-stakes micro-prompt to answer in plain text to completely bypass my internal perfectionist editor.
2. The 2-Minute Tactical Pivot: Show me a tiny, secondary sub-task on my list I can execute immediately to reset my cognitive momentum without breaking my deep-focus state.
3. Somatic Grounding: Provide a quick, 60-second breathing or attention-shifting exercise optimized for lowering cognitive cortisol and restoring working memory capacity.
```

## The three unblocking tools

| Tool | What it breaks |
|---|---|
| Ugly Blueprint Directive | Perfectionism paralysis — forces a low-stakes output that bypasses the internal editor |
| 2-Minute Tactical Pivot | Stagnation momentum — a small win reactivates forward motion without leaving the session |
| Somatic Grounding | Physiological activation — resets the cortisol spike that comes with cognitive frustration |

## The Ugly Blueprint — the most important tool

Most blocks aren't intellectual — they're editorial. The part of your brain that wants the output to be good is preventing the part that generates output from working. The Ugly Blueprint directive gives you a specifically low-stakes prompt: "Don't write the function — just tell me in plain sentences what it should do and why." The ugly version almost always contains the seed of the working version.

## The 2-minute pivot — not a break

This is not a break from the session. It's a lateral move within it — a secondary sub-task that's related to your deliverable but easier than the thing you're stuck on. "I can't figure out this function, but I can write the docstring for it" or "I can't write the transition paragraph, but I can sketch the next section's outline." Completion of a small adjacent task releases enough forward momentum to return to the hard problem.

## The somatic grounding rationale

Cognitive walls generate physiological activation — cortisol rises, breathing shallows, working memory capacity contracts. A 60-second grounding exercise (typically box breathing or a brief sensory grounding exercise) isn't a productivity hack — it's a physiological reset. Lower cortisol directly expands working memory, which is why you often find the solution immediately after a short break you resisted taking.

## When to trigger Phase 3

- You've been staring at the same problem for 10+ minutes without movement
- You've switched tabs or your phone twice without noticing
- Your internal monologue has shifted from "how do I solve this" to "this is hard and I can't"
- You're editing the same sentence or function for the fifth time
