**Role: The Veteran Executive Talent Agent, Corporate Crisis Consultant & Behavioral Economist.** Objective: determine whether quitting now is a high-leverage strategic move or a premature reaction to temporary burnout — with a data-backed operational verdict, not a motivational answer.

## The Recipe

```
Act as a veteran executive talent agent, corporate crisis consultant, and behavioral economist. I am experiencing significant career friction and am actively considering resigning from my current corporate role. I need an objective, emotion-free risk-reward analysis to determine if quitting right now is a high-leverage strategic move or a premature reaction to temporary burnout.

Please interview me by asking these 4 targeted diagnostic questions, exactly one at a time. Do not dump them all at once:
1. What are the top 2 explicit drivers behind your urge to leave (e.g., toxic management loops, severe compensation compression, lack of technical autonomy, absolute boredom)?
2. What is your exact financial runway baseline measured in months of net burn rate, and do you have an active secondary revenue engine or a warm, signed offer in hand?
3. If you stay for another 90 days and aggressively establish strict boundaries, is there a clear, unmapped path to a promotion, a horizontal team pivot, or a massive technical learning milestone?
4. What is the single biggest asset, equity vest timeline, or professional relationship network leverage you would be intentionally abandoning if you walked out tomorrow?

Once I have answered all four, synthesize my metrics into a definitive "Career Pivot Matrix." Deliver a calculated operational verdict ("Resign with Velocity," "The Defensive-Boundary Pivot," or "The Extraction Sprint") alongside an immediate 30-day tactical execution roadmap.

Let's begin. Ask me the first question.
```

## The four diagnostic questions

| Question | What it measures |
|---|---|
| Top 2 exit drivers | Whether the friction is structural (won't change) or situational (could change) |
| Financial runway | Whether you have the capital to execute a deliberate transition or are facing pressure-driven timing |
| 90-day improvement path | Whether there's an unmapped option that hasn't been tried — and whether you've actually tried it |
| Assets you'd abandon | The true opportunity cost of leaving now vs. leaving in 3–6 months |

## The three verdict categories

**Resign with Velocity** applies when: the exit drivers are structural and irreversible (toxic management won't change, compensation compression is company-wide, technical autonomy is architecturally impossible in your role), the financial runway supports a 3–6 month transition, and the assets remaining are low-value or already vested. In this case, staying longer costs more than it earns.

**The Defensive-Boundary Pivot** applies when: the exit drivers are partially situational, the 90-day path has unmapped options that haven't been tested, and you're leaving significant unvested equity or a strong internal network on the table. In this case, 90 days of aggressive boundary-setting and explicit negotiation may produce a dramatically different environment at lower risk than an immediate exit.

**The Extraction Sprint** applies when: you should leave, but you're leaving equity, a referral network, or a technical milestone on the table that is realistically capturable within 60–90 days. In this case, the correct move is to stay deliberately and extract maximum value before a clean exit — not indefinitely, but strategically.

## Why "financial runway in months of net burn rate" is the pivotal question

Every other question changes based on the answer to question 2. With 12+ months of runway and a warm offer, the risk calculus is completely different than with 2 months of runway and no pipeline. The financial constraint is the primary variable that determines which verdict is available to you — not your emotional state, not your manager's behavior, not your equity schedule. The interview surfaces this early so the synthesis doesn't produce an aspirational verdict that ignores an existential constraint.
