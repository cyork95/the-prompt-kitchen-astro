**Role: The Persuasive Copywriter.** Objective: write a cover letter that reads like it was written *for this company* — because it was.

## The Recipe

```
Act as an elite career strategist and persuasive copywriter. I need a short, high-impact cover letter for a company I am incredibly excited about. I want to completely banish generic, robotic boilerplate ("I am writing to express my interest...") and instead write an authentic, narrative-driven letter that proves deep domain alignment.

My target company and role: [INSERT COMPANY & ROLE]
The company's core mission or recent product launch: [INSERT CONTEXT / EMERGENCE]
My unique edge or professional background: [INSERT YOUR HIGHLIGHT]

Please write a 3-paragraph cover letter using this exact structural architecture:
- Paragraph 1 (The Non-Generic Hook): Start with a specific observation about a challenge, trend, or project the company is working on right now, instantly proving I actually research their space.
- Paragraph 2 (The Proof Point): Pivot to a high-leverage achievement from my past that proves I have already solved a parallel problem. Focus heavily on execution velocity and measurable metrics.
- Paragraph 3 (The Frictionless Close): Connect my personal engineering/professional philosophy to their culture and close with a direct, confident next step.

Keep the tone polished, authoritative, yet human and distinct.
```

## The three-paragraph architecture

| Paragraph | Job it does |
|---|---|
| Non-Generic Hook | Proves you know their world — earns the next 30 seconds of attention |
| Proof Point | Shows you've solved a parallel problem — converts attention into interest |
| Frictionless Close | Connects your philosophy to their culture — makes asking for a next step feel natural |

## Why the Hook must be specific

"I've long admired your company's commitment to innovation" gets deleted. "Your recent migration to a serverless event-driven architecture at scale is exactly the class of problem I've been building systems to solve for the last three years" gets read. The specificity is the proof that you're not mass-applying.

## What to put in the company context field

A recent product announcement, a blog post from their engineering team, a funding round and what it signals about their roadmap, a specific architectural challenge mentioned in a conference talk by their CTO. Anything that shows you did 20 minutes of actual research.
