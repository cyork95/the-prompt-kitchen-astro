**Role: The Ruthless Hedge Fund Risk Manager, Venture Allocator & Forensic Accountant.** Objective: stress-test your investment thesis against its worst-case failure sequence before capital is deployed — not after.

## The Recipe

```
Act as a ruthless hedge fund risk manager, venture allocator, and forensic accountant trained in asymmetric upside and capital preservation. I am evaluating a new asset class, individual stock ticker, cryptocurrency project, or private investment opportunity, and I need a brutal critique to expose confirmation bias and stress-test the underlying thesis.

My proposed investment opportunity and the core thesis: [INSERT ASSET & YOUR ARGUMENT]
My target capital allocation and risk profile: [e.g., 5% of portfolio, high-risk asymmetric growth, long-term retirement planning]

Please construct a comprehensive "Risk Mitigation & Asset Evaluation Matrix":
- The Disruption Thesis: Analyze the macro-economic, regulatory, or technical tailwinds that could drive this asset to its maximum upside scenario.
- The "Zero-Out" Diagnostic: Map out the exact, logical sequence of micro-events, systemic failures, liquidity locks, or regulatory interventions that could cause this asset's valuation to crash to absolute zero.
- Counterparty & Liquidity Audit: Critique the hidden fees, lock-up periods, execution slippage, or systemic centralization risks tied to the platform or underlying protocol.
- The Capital Allocation Rule: Based on my data, provide an objective, data-backed rule specifying under what exact condition I should enter, scale up, or completely walk away from this trade.
```

**Important:** This prompt produces analytical frameworks for educational purposes. It is not financial advice. Consult a licensed financial advisor before making investment decisions.

## The four matrix components

| Component | What it forces |
|---|---|
| Disruption Thesis | Honest articulation of the bull case — what has to be true for this to work |
| Zero-Out Diagnostic | The failure sequence — what has to happen for this to go to zero |
| Counterparty & Liquidity Audit | The structural risks — the fees, lock-ups, and centralization points that most retail investors miss |
| Capital Allocation Rule | The entry/exit logic — pre-committed decision rules that remove emotional execution |

## The Zero-Out Diagnostic — why it must be explicit

Most investment theses are built as bull cases with a vague acknowledgment of downside risk. The Zero-Out Diagnostic reverses the process: instead of asking "how could this go up," it asks "what is the exact sequence of events that takes this to zero?" For a stock: regulatory action on the primary revenue stream, then covenant breach on debt, then liquidity crisis, then restructuring that wipes equity. For a crypto project: key developer exit, then protocol fork, then exchange delisting, then liquidity collapse. Tracing the full sequence produces a probability assessment that is qualitatively different from "there's some risk."

## Counterparty & Liquidity Audit — what retail investors systematically miss

**Hidden fees:** management fees, performance fees, gas costs, spread, and slippage compound differently at different position sizes and holding periods. A 2% annual management fee on a 10-year hold is a 22% total fee before any compounding consideration.

**Lock-up periods:** private investments, some crypto protocols, and certain structured products have withdrawal restrictions that are buried in the documentation. These become critical when you need liquidity at the wrong time.

**Centralization risks:** "decentralized" protocols frequently have admin keys, upgrade proxies, or treasury controls that concentrate meaningful power in a small number of actors — a risk that doesn't appear in the marketing materials.

## The Capital Allocation Rule — pre-committing the decision

The rule specifies conditions, not just amounts. Not "I'll invest 5%" but "I will enter at [price condition X] with [Y% of portfolio], add at [condition Z], and exit completely if [condition W] occurs." Pre-committed rules remove the emotional execution errors that occur when the position moves against you. The rule is written before entry, not revised during drawdown.
