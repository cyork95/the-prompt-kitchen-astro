**Role: The Cognitive Performance Coach & Neurobiology Expert.** Objective: eliminate pre-session resistance — the fog, the friction, the unconscious delay — and drop directly into focused execution within three minutes.

## The Recipe

```
Act as a cognitive performance coach and neurobiology expert. I am ready to begin a high-focus Deep Work session, but I am feeling initial resistance, mental fog, or digital friction. I need a rapid-fire, 3-minute psychological protocol to drop my cognitive defenses and transition immediately into a flow state.

My single core task for this session is: [INSERT TASK]
My target focus time block is: [e.g., 90 minutes]

Please provide an immediate "Flow-State Trigger Protocol":
1. The 2-Minute Micro-Agreement: Give me a tiny, low-friction version of the first step of my task that is so small my brain cannot register it as an intimidating cognitive load.
2. The Sensory & Environment Lock: A 3-step checklist to lock down my physical and digital space right now to prevent attention switching.
3. The Internal Stopwatch: A mental framing statement to help me accept that the first 10 minutes will feel uncomfortable, and how to "surf" that friction until my focus engine catches.

Deliver the protocol cleanly so I can execute it and start my timer.
```

## The three trigger components

| Component | What it breaks |
|---|---|
| 2-Minute Micro-Agreement | Activation energy — the brain's resistance to starting is specifically about perceived task size, not the task itself |
| Sensory & Environment Lock | Attention switching — each open tab and notification is a competing intention waiting to fire |
| Internal Stopwatch | False exit — the first 10 minutes of any deep work session feel bad; most people mistake that for a signal to stop |

## The Micro-Agreement — why task size is the real barrier

The brain doesn't resist "working." It resists perceived cognitive load. "Write chapter 3" triggers resistance because the brain maps the full scope. "Open the document and write one sentence" doesn't. The Micro-Agreement exploits this by offering the brain a commitment so small it can't justify refusing — and once the document is open and one sentence exists, the sunk cost logic flips: continuing feels easier than stopping.

## The Sensory & Environment Lock — what "locked down" means specifically

A generic "remove distractions" instruction produces nothing because it leaves every decision open. The Lock is a concrete 3-step checklist: phone physically removed from arm's reach (not silenced — removed), all browser tabs except the working document closed, and a physical signal that you've entered session mode (headphones on, door closed, status set). Each step removes a specific vector for attention switching, not just a general category of distraction.

## The Internal Stopwatch — surfing the discomfort window

The first 8–12 minutes of focused work reliably feel worse than not working. Attention is scattered, the task feels harder than expected, and the pull toward checking something is strong. Most people interpret this as a signal that focus isn't coming. It isn't — it's the transition cost. The framing statement makes this explicit: "This discomfort is not a warning. It is the tunnel before the focus engine catches. I will not exit the tunnel early." Knowing the discomfort has a defined end makes it navigable.
