**Role: The Performance Coach.** Objective: convert your actual career experience into a structured, metrics-anchored story that sounds natural under interview pressure.

## The Recipe

```
Act as an executive performance coach and master storyteller. I want to prep for behavioral interview questions using the STAR framework (Situation, Task, Action, Result). I need my answers to sound structured, high-agency, and metrics-focused without sounding rehearsed or artificial.

Please interview me one question at a time to extract a core professional story from my career. Do not dump the questions all at once. Over our dialogue, help me map out:
1. The Crucible (Situation & Task): What was a complex, high-stakes project, conflict, or failure you faced? What were the explicit technical or business constraints?
2. Your Direct Leverage (Action): What *specific* steps did you personally take to unblock the team, refactor the process, or build the solution? Focus heavily on your autonomy and systems-thinking.
3. The Undeniable ROI (Result): What was the quantifiable outcome? Express this in concrete metrics (e.g., system latency reduced by 40%, saved $50k in compute costs, shipped 3 weeks ahead of schedule).

Once we finish the dialogue, compile this story into a polished, scannable "STAR Matrix Profile" that I can quickly review right before my interview.

Let's begin. Ask me about the initial situation or project.
```

## The three STAR extraction layers

| Layer | What it forces |
|---|---|
| The Crucible (Situation & Task) | Specificity of context — a real high-stakes scenario, not a generic project summary |
| Your Direct Leverage (Action) | First-person agency — what *you* specifically did, not what "the team" did |
| The Undeniable ROI (Result) | Metrics — a number, a percentage, a timeline, a dollar amount |

## Why "without sounding rehearsed" is the goal

The failure mode of STAR prep is robotic delivery — you've memorized the answer so thoroughly it loses all natural texture. The one-question-at-a-time interview format forces you to *reconstruct* the story in conversation, which is exactly what you'll do in a real interview. The profile you get at the end is a reference, not a script.

## The "direct leverage" trap

Most candidates describe what the team accomplished. Interviewers are evaluating *you* — they want to hear "I proposed re-architecting the ingestion layer, which I owned end-to-end" not "we decided to refactor the pipeline." This layer specifically asks for your personal contribution because that's what the interview is testing.

## Build multiple profiles

Run this recipe 3–4 times on different career stories. Behavioral interviews typically ask "tell me about a time when..." across multiple domains: technical challenge, conflict, failure, leadership, ambiguity. Having a matrix profile for each scenario ready to reference gives you confident, specific answers to any angle the interviewer takes.
