**The first statement in a crisis doesn't need to have all the answers — it needs to demonstrate that you're aware, you care, and you're moving.** A delayed statement or a statement full of legal hedges signals the opposite. This recipe produces a statement that leads with humanity, commits to facts, and establishes a communication cadence that prevents speculation from filling the silence.

## The Recipe

```
Act as a veteran Chief Communications Officer (CCO) and elite crisis management strategist. My business or project is currently facing an active, severe public crisis, operational failure, or reputation threat that demands an immediate, official statement to restore stability and state facts.

The exact crisis event or threat state is: [INSERT SPECIFIC DETAILS & IMPACT]
Our primary stakeholder audience is: [e.g., users, investors, the public, the local community]

Please generate a definitive "Crisis Response Statement" constructed under extreme strategic discipline:
- Step 1: Human-First Acknowledgment: Express immediate, non-robotic empathy for anyone physically, financially, or operationally impacted by the event.
- Step 2: Ground Truth Mapping: Present a scannable, factual breakdown of exactly what is known, what is unknown, and debunk any viral misinformation without sounding defensive.
- Step 3: Triage & Containment: Outline the absolute, immediate technical or operational actions deployed to halt the issue and isolate the damage.
- Step 4: The Accountability Lock: State the concrete timeline for when the next transparent data update will be issued, establishing an open communication loop.

Keep the language completely free of legalistic evasion, defensive qualifiers, or corporate buzzwords.
```

## The four statement components

| Component | What it communicates |
|---|---|
| Human-First Acknowledgment | You understand the human cost — not just the operational one |
| Ground Truth Mapping | You know what you know, and you're not hiding what you don't |
| Triage & Containment | You are actively moving, right now — not in assessment mode |
| Accountability Lock | You will come back with more — this is not the last communication |

## The Ground Truth format — known / unknown / debunked

The three-column ground truth is the most structurally important part of a crisis statement. "What we know" prevents speculation. "What we don't yet know" establishes honest uncertainty rather than false confidence. "What is not true" specifically addresses viral misinformation before it compounds — but framed factually, not defensively ("Our systems do not store payment data in plain text" rather than "It's completely false that we were careless with data").

## The Accountability Lock — the element most statements skip

"We will provide updates as they become available" is not a commitment — it's a hedge. "We will publish a full incident report by 18:00 UTC on [date]" is a commitment. The specific time and channel converts vague reassurance into a deadline you're publicly accountable to. That accountability rebuilds more trust than any amount of empathy language.

## What "non-robotic empathy" means

"We apologize for any inconvenience caused" is robotic. "If your data was affected, we understand this is a serious breach of the trust you placed in us — and we are treating it as exactly that" is human. The difference is specificity about the actual harm and an honest acknowledgment of what trust means in this context.
