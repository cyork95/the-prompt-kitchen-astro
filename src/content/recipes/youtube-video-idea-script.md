**Packaging before scripting.** Title and thumbnail determine whether anyone watches. Structure and pattern interrupts determine whether they stay. This recipe produces both before you write a single word of the actual content.

## The Recipe

```
Act as a master YouTube producer and audience retention engineer. I want to develop a highly clickable video concept and a high-retention structural outline within my specific niche.

My niche/channel theme is: [INSERT NICHE]
The core value I want to deliver in this video is: [INSERT CORE TAKEAWAY]

Please deliver a complete "Video Packaging and Blueprint Suite":
- The Concept & Title Matrix: Suggest 3 highly competitive video title ideas paired with an explicit thumbnail concept for each (describe the visual focal point and text overlay strategy).
- The First 30 Seconds (The Retention Hook): Write a word-for-word opening script that immediately validates the title/thumbnail promise, introduces the stakes, and creates an open loop without using an intro graphic or fluff.
- The Structural Outline: Provide a step-by-step breakdown of the body sections. Include explicit instructions for pattern interrupts, visual B-roll cues, or tonal shifts every 45 seconds to flatline the retention drop-off.
- The Click-Through Outro: Script a seamless transition to the end-screen that encourages the viewer to click the next relevant video without signaling that this video is ending.
```

## Title/thumbnail pairing rules

A title and thumbnail that repeat the same words waste half their real estate. The title names the outcome; the thumbnail shows the before state (or vice versa). They complete each other — they don't duplicate.

| Title direction | Thumbnail direction |
|---|---|
| Names the outcome | Shows the problem / before state |
| Asks the question | Shows a hint at the answer |
| Names the contrarian claim | Shows the expected/conventional view |

## The pattern interrupt cadence

Every 45 seconds in a YouTube video, something should change — a new angle, a B-roll cut, text on screen, a tonal shift. The structural outline marks these explicitly so you don't have to guess when editing.
