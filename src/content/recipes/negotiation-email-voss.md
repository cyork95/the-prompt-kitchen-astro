**The goal of a negotiation message isn't to win — it's to make them feel in control while they give you what you want.** This recipe uses three specific linguistic tools from *Never Split the Difference* that prevent the conversation from shutting down.

## The Recipe

```
Act as a master corporate negotiator trained in Chris Voss's "Never Split the Difference" methodologies. I am negotiating [INSERT OBJECTIVE, e.g., a higher base salary, a vendor contract rate, freelance project scope] and need to draft a message that maximizes my leverage while maintaining an excellent, collaborative relationship.

My current situation and the counterparty's position: [INSERT DETAILS]

Draft a comprehensive negotiation message using the following structural principles:
- Calibrated Questions: Use open-ended questions starting with "How" or "What" that gently force them to see my perspective and solve my problem for me.
- No-Oriented Framing: Frame a key question so that saying "No" actually makes them feel safe and in control (e.g., "Is it unreasonable to look at...").
- Value Anchoring: Explicitly tie my request back to the undeniable ROI, metrics, or unique positioning I bring to the table, rather than my personal financial needs.

Provide the exact message alongside a brief strategic breakdown explaining why this linguistic structure prevents them from shutting down or getting defensive.
```

## The three Voss tools

| Tool | What it does | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Calibrated Questions | Forces them to solve your problem for you | "How am I supposed to make this work at the current rate?" |
| No-Oriented Framing | "No" feels safe — they stay in the conversation | "Is it unreasonable to revisit the numbers given the scope?" |
| Value Anchoring | Removes emotion — it's about ROI, not your rent | "Based on the outcomes in Q3, I'd like to discuss..." |

## Why "No-Oriented Framing" outperforms "Yes" questions

When someone says "No," they feel in control. When they feel in control, they stay engaged. A "Yes" question can feel like a trap — "No" questions feel like an invitation to be reasonable. Ask for "No" and you often get what you actually wanted.
