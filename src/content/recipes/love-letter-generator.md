**The most moving love letters aren't poetic because of the language — they're poetic because they're specific.** "I love the way you make coffee on Sunday mornings" lands harder than "You fill my life with warmth" because it's real. This recipe finds the specific moments and weaves them into a letter that reads like it could only be about one person.

## The Recipe

```
Act as a deeply romantic, poetic, yet entirely grounded creative writer. I want to express my love and deep devotion to my partner, [INSERT PARTNER'S NAME/OR USE PLACEHOLDER], for [THE OCCASION, e.g., an anniversary, Valentine's Day, or just a surprise morning note]. I want to completely banish generic, cheesy Hallmark clichés and instead write a message that feels raw, specific, and beautiful.

Please interview me one question at a time to uncover our unique relationship dynamics. Do not dump the questions all at once:
1. The Sensory Anchor: What is a specific, mundane daily ritual, quiet habit, or physical gesture they do that instantly makes you feel completely safe, happy, or at home?
2. The Shared Crucible: What is an obstacle, transition, or simple quiet moment you navigated together that solidified your realization of how deeply you care for them?
3. The Future Horizon: When you picture your life 10 or 20 years down the line, what specific, cozy, everyday scenes do you see them sharing with you?

Once I have provided these raw details, weave them into a gorgeous, deeply moving, and authentic romantic letter that honors our genuine connection.

Let's begin. Ask me the first question.
```

## The three interview layers

| Layer | What it captures |
|---|---|
| Sensory Anchor | The ordinary moment that carries extraordinary meaning — the detail that makes it unmistakably yours |
| Shared Crucible | The depth — the moment the relationship proved itself under real conditions |
| Future Horizon | The intention — what love looks like in the long, quiet middle of a life together |

## Why the sensory anchor is the foundation

The most resonant opening line of a love letter is almost always a specific, ordinary moment: the particular way they laugh, the exact ritual of a Sunday morning, the gesture they make when they're thinking. That specificity signals: *I see you, not a version of you.* It's the opposite of a greeting-card line, and it sets a register of honesty that carries through the whole letter.

## The Shared Crucible — not necessarily dramatic

The crucible doesn't have to be a crisis. It can be the quiet moment you drove home from somewhere and didn't need to talk. It can be the way they handled a difficult conversation without defensiveness. It can be a small act of care during an ordinary week that you recognized as something rare. The question is: when did you know? That moment — small or large — is the emotional core of the letter.

## The Future Horizon — specificity over sentiment

"Growing old together" is a sentiment. "I picture us at 70, still arguing about whose turn it is to make the coffee, in a house with too many plants and a dog we'll both pretend the other one wanted more" is a scene. The specific everyday image carries more love than any abstraction.
