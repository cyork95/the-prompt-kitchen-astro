**Over-apologizing is a boundary violation against yourself.** "I'm so sorry, but..." signals that the boundary is negotiable. This recipe writes the message that isn't — in three tones, so you can match the relationship.

## The Recipe

```
Act as a professional communications consultant specializing in workplace boundaries and burnout prevention. I need to set a firm, polite, and unshakeable boundary with [INSERT RECIPIENT, e.g., a demanding client, a manager, a cross-functional partner] who is pushing past standard professional expectations.

The specific boundary infraction is: [INSERT SITUATION, e.g., scope creep without budget updates, messaging late at night expecting immediate replies, assigning tasks over a planned vacation].

Generate 3 distinct communication swipes ranging in tone from collaborative to highly formal:
- Version 1 (The Collaborative Pivot): Offers an alternative solution or timeline while protecting my current time/scope.
- Version 2 (The Direct/Professional): Clear, explicit boundary-setting that uses objective company policy or pre-arranged agreements as the anchor point.
- Version 3 (The Brief/Asynchronous): A minimal, zero-explanation message designed to acknowledge their input while deferring the execution to a standard, sustainable time window.

All versions must remain entirely professional, polite, and free of over-apologizing (eliminate phrases like "Sorry for the inconvenience" or "I'm so sorry, but...").
```

## The three versions and when to use each

| Version | Tone | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| Collaborative Pivot | Warm, solution-oriented | First offense, valued relationship, room to negotiate |
| Direct/Professional | Neutral, policy-anchored | Repeat offense, formal relationship, needs documentation |
| Brief/Async | Minimal, no-explanation | After hours messages, low-trust context, needs to end the thread |

## The over-apologizing trap

"Sorry for the inconvenience" signals that your boundary is causing a problem you're responsible for. It isn't. Replace it with neutral acknowledgment: "I've received your message and will address it [on Tuesday / within scope / during business hours]." No apology. No explanation. Full stop.
