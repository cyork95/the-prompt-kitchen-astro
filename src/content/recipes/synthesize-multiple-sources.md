**Having five sources doesn't mean you have clarity — it often means you have five conflicting framings.** This recipe processes them into a single coherent picture: the consensus, the divergences, and the gap all sources missed.

## The Recipe

```
Act as a senior knowledge management specialist and research synthesizer. I have collected multiple viewpoints, raw notes, or excerpts from different sources regarding a single complex issue, and I need an elegant, unified synthesis that highlights overlapping patterns and hidden tensions.

Please ask me to paste my raw source texts one by one or all at once. Once I provide them, process the information into a single "Synthesized Intelligence Dossier":
- The Core Consensus: Identify the fundamental truths, data points, or conclusions that *all* sources completely agree on.
- The Friction Points (The Divergences): Map out where the sources explicitly or implicitly contradict each other. Explain *why* their perspectives differ (e.g., differing underlying methodologies, mismatched incentives, or contrasting philosophical frameworks).
- The Missing Dimension: What critical perspective, edge case, or systemic factor did *none* of these sources account for?

Tell me how to paste my sources so you can begin the synthesis engine.
```

## The three dossier sections

| Section | What it reveals |
|---|---|
| Core Consensus | The reliable foundation — what you can build on with confidence |
| Friction Points | Where the sources diverge and *why* — methodology, incentives, framing |
| Missing Dimension | The blind spot the entire discourse shares — often the most valuable insight |

## Why "why they diverge" matters more than "that they diverge"

Two sources disagreeing about the same dataset isn't just a conflict — it's a clue. Different conclusions from the same data usually mean different underlying frameworks, different definitions of success, or different incentive structures. The synthesis maps those root causes, not just the surface disagreement.

## The Missing Dimension section

This is often the highest-value output. When all sources share the same blind spot, it usually indicates: a dominant paradigm nobody is questioning, a constituency whose perspective isn't represented, or a second-order effect nobody has traced yet. That gap is frequently where the most interesting work lives.
