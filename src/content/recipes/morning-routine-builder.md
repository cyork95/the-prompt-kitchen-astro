**A morning routine that only works on perfect days isn't a routine — it's an aspiration.** This recipe designs three versions for three real scenarios, so you have a protocol for the good days, the rushed ones, and the ones where everything goes sideways.

## The Recipe

```
Act as a human performance coach and circadian biology expert. I want to design a realistic, low-friction morning routine that optimizes my cortisol awakening response, builds immediate cognitive momentum, and prepares me for deep focus.

Please design a timeline-based morning architecture for three distinct scenarios:
- The "Ideal Day" Version (30-45 minutes): An optimal routine featuring light exposure, hydration, movement, and a zero-device mindfulness/planning window.
- The "Compressed" Version (10-15 minutes): A rapid, high-leverage sequence to run when I wake up late but still need to lock in my biological and mental baselines.
- The "Emergency" Variant (3 minutes): The absolute bare minimum physical actions to take to reset my state when life completely derails the morning.

Ensure this plan is entirely free of toxic, over-engineered productivity hype — make it grounded, practical, and highly restorative.
```

## The three versions at a glance

| Version | Time | When to use |
|---|---|---|
| Ideal Day | 30–45 min | You woke up on time and have space |
| Compressed | 10–15 min | Late start but want to set a baseline |
| Emergency | 3 min | Alarm failed, chaos started immediately |

## The science behind the structure

The cortisol awakening response (CAR) naturally peaks 30–45 minutes after waking — this is your biological alert signal. Light exposure amplifies it. Hydration supports it. Immediately reaching for your phone blunts it. The Ideal Day routine is built around working with this window, not against it.

## The "no productivity hype" constraint

Keep it in the prompt. Without it, AI-generated morning routines tend toward: cold plunge, 90-minute workout, journaling in a leather-bound notebook, and meditation by 6:30 AM. The constraint forces practical, actually-restorative recommendations instead.
