**Most people don't burn out suddenly — they drift out, gradually misallocating time and energy until the gap between what they're doing and what matters becomes too wide to ignore.** This 15-minute audit catches that drift weekly, before it compounds.

## The Recipe

```
Act as an empathetic but incredibly rigorous high-performance executive coach. I want to run a 15-minute "Weekly Life Audit" to evaluate my time allocation, ensure my daily actions align with my long-term values, and prevent burnout before it happens.

Please guide me through this review by asking me the following questions across 3 sequential phases, one phase at a time (do not dump all the questions at once):
- Phase 1: The Energy Audit. (What single activity or relationship drained your energy the most this week? What activity gave you a state of pure flow?)
- Phase 2: The Time Tracking Truths. (Review your real calendar or notes. Where did you fall into "fake work" or administrative procrastination to avoid high-leverage tasks?)
- Phase 3: The Upcoming Pivot. (What is the single highest-impact domino task for the next 7 days? What is one non-essential project or boundary infraction you are explicitly committing to saying "No" to?)

Let's begin. Ask me the Phase 1 questions.
```

## The three audit phases

| Phase | Core question |
|---|---|
| Energy Audit | Where did your energy go this week — drained vs. flow state? |
| Time Tracking Truths | What was fake work, and what was the high-leverage task you avoided? |
| Upcoming Pivot | What's your single highest-impact move, and what are you explicitly declining? |

## Phase 2 — the "fake work" diagnosis

Fake work is real activity that creates the *feeling* of productivity without meaningfully advancing your goals: reorganizing files, over-communicating status updates, attending meetings you don't need to be in, refining work that's already good enough. It's not laziness — it's anxiety-driven busyness that crowds out the harder, higher-leverage work. Naming it specifically (not "I was busy") is the whole exercise.

## The single highest-impact domino

Not a list of next week's priorities. One specific task that — if completed — makes the most other things easier or unlocks the most forward movement. If you can name it easily, you probably already know you've been avoiding it.

## The explicit No commitment

A vague intention to "be more protective of my time" doesn't change behavior. An explicit commitment — "I am saying No to [specific project/request/habit] this week" — does. The more specific and uncomfortable the answer, the more useful the audit.
