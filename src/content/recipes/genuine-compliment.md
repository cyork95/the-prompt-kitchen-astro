**Generic compliments are forgotten in hours. Specific ones land differently.** "You're so smart" is pleasant. "I noticed you always wait until everyone has spoken before you synthesize — and it makes people feel like their input actually shaped the conclusion" is the kind of thing someone remembers for years. This recipe finds the specific observation and turns it into both formats.

## The Recipe

```
Act as an insightful interpersonal psychologist and master wordsmith. I want to write a deeply meaningful, unforgettable compliment for [INSERT PERSON & RELATIONSHIP] to recognize their impact. I want to completely bypass shallow, generic flattery ("You're so smart," "Great job") and instead focus on character, unique behavior, and non-obvious strengths.

Please interview me one question at a time to gather the raw primitives of this compliment. Do not dump all the questions at once:
1. The Micro-Observation: What is a specific, small action, unique habit, or way they handle situations that you've quietly noticed they do exceptionally well?
2. The Downstream Impact: How does that specific action or trait positively impact you, the team, or the environment around them?
3. The Unseen Effort: What hidden dedication, sacrifice, or emotional labor do you think they put into this trait that most people completely take for granted?

Once I have answered all three, synthesize my data into 2 distinct versions:
- Version 1: A brief, punchy, informal slack/text message format that balances lighthearted warmth with deep sincerity.
- Version 2: A structured, high-impact paragraph optimized for a formal card, performance review shoutout, or letter of appreciation.

Let's begin. Ask me the first question.
```

## The three interview layers

| Layer | What it surfaces |
|---|---|
| Micro-Observation | The specific behavior — what they actually *do*, not a general trait |
| Downstream Impact | Why it matters — how it affects others, not just the fact that it's admirable |
| Unseen Effort | The recognition of the invisible cost — the element that makes the compliment feel deeply seen |

## Why the Unseen Effort layer matters most

Generic compliments acknowledge results. Meaningful compliments acknowledge the effort behind results. When you name the invisible work — "I imagine holding space for everyone's opinion in a high-pressure room takes more discipline than it appears" — you're telling someone that you see the cost of their excellence, not just the output. That recognition lands at a different level than any praise for the outcome alone.

## The two format versions

**Slack/text (Version 1):** Conversational, warm, and brief. Something that reads naturally in a message thread without sounding stiff or overly formal. Still specific — just lighter in tone.

**Formal paragraph (Version 2):** Full-length, structured for a card, a review shoutout, or a written letter. Carries the weight appropriate for a documented moment of recognition.
