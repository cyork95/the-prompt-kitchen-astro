**A gift that makes someone feel deeply seen requires information, not inspiration.** This recipe interviews you about the recipient — one question at a time — then generates 5 specific ideas ranging from tangible objects to curated experiences, each with a clear rationale for why it fits.

## The Recipe

```
Act as a master gift-giving consultant and thoughtful curator. I want to find a hyper-personalized, memorable gift for someone important to me. I want to completely avoid generic gifts (like basic gift cards, generic mugs, or cheap gift baskets) and find something that makes them feel deeply seen.

Please interview me one question at a time to gather background details. Do not dump all the questions at once. Over our dialogue, uncover:
1. The Recipient & Occasion: Who are they to you, and what is the milestone?
2. Their Consistently Tracked Passions/Obsessions: What are their active hobbies, specialized tools they use, daily rituals (e.g., tea tasting journals, vinyl collecting), or favorite sub-genres of media?
3. The Nostalgia/Inside Joke Vault: Are there shared memories, a specific city they love, or an inside joke that holds emotional weight?
4. Your Budgetary Boundary: What is the target spending tier?

Once I have answered all four, generate 5 highly specific, tiered gift concepts (ranging from tangible objects to unique curated experiences) alongside an exact rationale for why this matches their specific identity.

Let's begin. Ask me the first question.
```

## The four interview dimensions

| Dimension | What it unlocks |
|---|---|
| Recipient & Occasion | The baseline context — who this is for and how meaningful it needs to feel |
| Passions & Obsessions | The specific niche within their interest (not "loves cooking" — loves a specific technique) |
| Nostalgia & Inside Jokes | The emotional resonance layer — gifts that reference shared history hit differently than gifts that are just nice objects |
| Budget | The tier determines whether ideas are objects, experiences, commissions, or subscriptions |

## Why one question at a time

Getting all four questions at once produces shallow answers — you scan the list and give quick responses. One question at a time creates space to actually think. The nostalgia question especially rewards a pause: "What's a shared memory that would make them smile if it showed up in a gift?" takes longer to answer well than it seems.

## The 5 tiered concepts

The output spans the spectrum from under $30 to meaningful splurge — you choose the tier that fits. The rationale column is critical: "because you said they journal about their tea practice and this notebook is designed specifically for sensory tasting notes" is more useful than "they'll love it."
