**Most decision-making stops at first-order consequences — the obvious, intended payoffs.** Second and third-order thinking maps what happens next: how systems react, how people adapt, and what new problems your solution creates. This recipe runs that analysis before you commit.

## The Recipe

```
Act as a systems engineer, strategic risk analyst, and master futurist. I am considering making a major decision, policy change, or strategic move, and I want to look past the immediate, obvious benefits to run a rigorous "Second-Order Thinking Exercise." I need to map out the cascading, long-term, and unintended consequences of my actions.

My proposed decision or change is: [INSERT DECISION HERE]

Please analyze my choice and break your systemic projection down into three distinct temporal waves:
- First-Order Consequences (The immediate, intentional, surface-level payoffs and changes).
- Second-Order Consequences (The ripples: How will other people, systems, dependencies, or competitor behaviors react to the first-order changes? What new problems will the initial solution create?).
- Third-Order Consequences & Systems Feedback Loops (The endgame: 12-24 months out, what complex, reinforcing, or balancing loops will stabilize or destabilize this decision? What is the hidden existential trap?).

Provide a clean markdown systems diagram or table summarizing these downstream ripples so I can make a defensive, resilient choice.
```

## The three temporal waves

| Wave | Timeframe | Question to ask |
|---|---|---|
| First-Order | Immediately | What do I intend to happen? |
| Second-Order | Weeks to months | How will others react to what I intended? |
| Third-Order | 12–24 months | What feedback loops does the reaction create? |

## Why second-order thinking changes decisions

Most bad outcomes weren't unforeseeable — they were just unconsidered. "We cut costs by reducing support staff" (first order: lower costs) → "customers receive slower support and churn increases" (second order) → "revenue falls below the level the cost cut saved" (third order: the decision was net negative).

Running the cascade before deciding is the difference between strategy and reaction.

## The hidden existential trap

The third-order question — "what is the hidden existential trap?" — specifically looks for the feedback loop that could compound against you. Not just bad outcomes, but self-reinforcing bad outcomes. That's the scenario worth planning a defensive exit for before you start.
