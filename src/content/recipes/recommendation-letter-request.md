**The recommendation request that makes writing the letter easy gets a better letter.** Most writers go blank when asked — they know you well but can't immediately summon three pages of specific examples. This recipe solves that by building the cheat sheet directly into the request, so the writer can focus on expressing the relationship rather than reconstructing the timeline.

## The Recipe

```
Act as a polished professional advocate and career transition coach. I need to request a formal letter of recommendation from a past [INSERT ROLE, e.g., manager, engineering tech lead, college professor] for an upcoming opportunity [INSERT OPPORTUNITY, e.g., a senior data engineering role, a specialized academic program]. I want to make the request entirely low-friction, respectful of their time, and easy for them to say yes to.

My history/relationship with this person: [INSERT DETAILS, e.g., worked under them for 2 years at X company, shipped Y data pipeline project]
The key skills/traits I need this letter to highlight: [e.g., high technical agency, architectural leadership, handling ambiguity]

Please write a highly professional request email including:
- The Gracious Re-entry: A warm, authentic opening that briefly updates them on my career trajectory without dragging on.
- The Clear Scope: State exactly what the recommendation is for, why their specific perspective is uniquely valuable to this new path, and the definitive deadline date.
- The "Cheat Sheet" Asset: Provide a scannable, bulleted layout placeholder within the email where I can summarize the top 2 projects we shipped together and the exact metrics achieved, making it effortless for them to write the letter without doing memory digging.
- The Clean Out: A polite closing line that allows them an elegant way to decline if their current schedule or bandwidth is completely maxed out.
```

## The four email components

| Component | What it does |
|---|---|
| Gracious Re-entry | Re-establishes the relationship before asking anything |
| Clear Scope | Removes ambiguity — what, why them specifically, by when |
| Cheat Sheet Asset | Does the memory work for them — projects, metrics, and traits already named |
| Clean Out | Removes the awkward decline — gives them a graceful path without social cost |

## The Cheat Sheet — the highest-value element

Most recommendation requests end with "let me know if you need anything from me." That's insufficient. The writer needs: the two or three projects that are most relevant to the new opportunity, the specific metrics from each, and the exact qualities you want the letter to emphasize. Providing this reduces their work from "compose from scratch" to "confirm and expand." That directly produces a better letter in less time.

## What to put in the Cheat Sheet

```
For reference, the two projects I'd find most relevant to highlight:

1. [Project Name] — [one-sentence description]. Outcome: [specific metric, e.g., reduced latency by 40%, shipped 3 weeks early].

2. [Project Name] — [one-sentence description]. Outcome: [specific metric].

Key qualities I'm hoping the letter can speak to: [e.g., technical ownership, architectural thinking, cross-functional communication].
```

## The Clean Out line — why it's essential

"I completely understand if your current bandwidth doesn't allow for this" gives the writer a graceful exit. Without it, a "no" requires them to compose an explanation and manage your potential disappointment. With it, a quick "my schedule is maxed right now, but best of luck" is easy to send — and you've preserved the relationship. Writers who can't do it now might enthusiastically do it next time.
