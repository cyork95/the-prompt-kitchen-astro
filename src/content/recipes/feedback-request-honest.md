**Most feedback requests produce compliments, not data.** "How am I doing?" is unanswerable honestly — the social cost of a real answer is too high. This recipe removes that barrier by naming your own vulnerability first, asking questions with behavioral specificity that can't be deflected, and signaling clearly that honesty is what you actually want.

## The Recipe

```
Act as an elite executive coach and organizational trust researcher. I want to ask my [INSERT RECIPIENT, e.g., direct reports, manager, freelance clients] for constructive feedback on my performance, communication, and leadership. I need a message that completely eliminates the standard "politeness barrier" and creates absolute psychological safety so they give me raw, actionable truths instead of generic compliments.

Draft a feedback request email/message utilizing the following structural levers:
- The Vulnerability Anchor: Lead by acknowledging a specific area where I know I have been experimenting, pushing fast, or could improve (e.g., "I've been trying to streamline our sync meetings...").
- Specific, Limited Scope: Avoid broad questions like "How am I doing?" Generate 2-3 hyper-focused, open-ended questions that ask for explicit behavioral data (e.g., "What is one thing I do in meetings that accidentally creates friction for you?").
- Explicit Protection: Write a closing line that reinforces that critical feedback is the highest-value asset they can give me and will be met with zero defensiveness.

Provide the exact message text along with a quick operational tip on how to handle their responses gracefully.
```

## The three structural levers

| Lever | What it removes |
|---|---|
| Vulnerability Anchor | The power asymmetry — you go first, which makes honesty safer |
| Specific, Limited Scope | The "how am I doing?" deflection — behavioral questions can't be answered with platitudes |
| Explicit Protection | The anticipatory defensiveness — you explicitly name what you'll do with the answer |

## The Vulnerability Anchor — why it works

Leading with "I've been experimenting with X and I'm genuinely uncertain if it's landing well" does three things: it signals self-awareness, it identifies a specific area worth commenting on, and it reduces the social risk of the response. The person no longer has to be the one to raise the uncomfortable observation — you already raised it. They just have to confirm or redirect.

## Behavioral questions vs. evaluative questions

| Evaluative (avoid) | Behavioral (use) |
|---|---|
| "Am I communicating clearly?" | "Is there a moment in our last project where my communication created confusion for you?" |
| "How is my leadership?" | "What's one thing I do in 1:1s that doesn't feel as useful as it could?" |
| "Am I a good manager?" | "What decision did I make recently that you wish I'd involved you in earlier?" |

Behavioral questions have specific answers. Evaluative questions have social answers.

## The operational tip

The message includes advice on how to receive the responses — which is often the skill that determines whether people give honest feedback the *next* time you ask. Responding with gratitude and no counter-argument to critical feedback, then acting visibly on one piece of it, converts a one-time feedback request into a relationship where honest input flows naturally.
