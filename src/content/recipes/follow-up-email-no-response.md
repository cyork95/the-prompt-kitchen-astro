**"Just checking in" is the fastest way to get ignored again.** The follow-up that works either lowers friction, adds new value, or triggers loss aversion. This recipe gives you all three — pick the one that fits the relationship.

## The Recipe

```
Act as an expert in business communication and behavioral psychology. I sent an important email a few days ago regarding [INSERT TOPIC/GOAL] to [INSERT RECIPIENT ROLE/RELATIONSHIP] and have received no response. I need a follow-up email that triggers a reply without sounding passive-aggressive, desperate, or annoying.

Please provide 3 distinct follow-up variations based on different psychological triggers:
1. The "Clean Bump" (Short, low-friction, preserves their mental bandwidth).
2. The "Alternative Value Drop" (Adding a quick piece of relevant news, a resource, or an insight to make replying high-value for them).
3. The "Lines in the Sand" Breakaway (A polite, final email that gives them an easy out, which frequently triggers a response due to loss aversion).

Ensure all versions remain professional, brief, and completely eliminate empty filler phrases like "Just checking in" or "Per my last email."
```

## The three follow-up triggers

| Version | Psychology | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| Clean Bump | Reduces cognitive load — easy to reply to | Busy executives, casual relationships |
| Value Drop | Makes replying feel rewarding, not obligatory | Warm prospects, partnerships |
| Breakaway | Loss aversion — giving an easy "out" triggers engagement | Cold leads, long silences |
