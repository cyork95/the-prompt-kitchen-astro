**The conversation you avoid costs more than the one you have.** This recipe scripts the hardest part — the first 60 seconds and the moment it goes sideways — so you're not improvising when your adrenaline is up.

## The Recipe

```
Act as an expert corporate mediator and interpersonal psychologist specializing in the "Crucial Conversations" framework. I need to have a high-stakes, highly sensitive difficult conversation with [INSERT PERSON'S ROLE, e.g., my manager, a direct report, a co-founder] regarding [INSERT SENSITIVE ISSUE, e.g., missed deadlines, toxic communication style, misaligned expectations].

Help me script and prep for this conversation by providing:
1. The Opening Frame (The first 60 seconds): How to state the facts of the issue clearly without attributing negative motives, establishing a "Safe Space" immediately.
2. The State-My-Path Script: A word-for-word talk track using "I" statements that balances deep candor with psychological safety.
3. The Pivot to Listening: How to transition the monologue into a collaborative dialogue, inviting them to share their perspective safely.
4. The Boundary Deflector: Provide 2 specific verbal scripts I can use to gently steer the conversation back on track if they become overly defensive, emotional, or try to shift blame.
```

## The four parts of the script

| Part | Goal | Common failure mode |
|---|---|---|
| Opening Frame | State facts, no motives attributed | Starting with "You always..." |
| State-My-Path | Candor with safety — "I" not "You" | Hedging so much the point gets lost |
| Pivot to Listening | Make it a conversation, not a lecture | Asking "Do you understand?" instead of "What's your view?" |
| Boundary Deflector | Redirect without escalating | Matching their emotional temperature |

## The opening frame rule

State observable behavior, not character. "I've noticed the last three deliverables came in after the agreed date" lands differently than "You keep missing deadlines." One is a fact. The other is a verdict.
