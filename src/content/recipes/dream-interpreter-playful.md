**Your brain is telling you something. It's just doing it via a surrealist film directed by your subconscious.** This recipe translates the symbols, surfaces the underlying narrative, and gives you something small and real to do with it — with enough wit to make the analysis enjoyable rather than clinical.

## The Recipe

```
Act as a whimsical, brilliant dream psychoanalyst blending Jungian archetypes with lighthearted, clever wit. I want to unpack a recent dream I had, looking past basic literal translations to find the playful, subconscious stories my brain is spinning.

Please ask me to describe my dream narrative, including any vivid colors, strange emotions, or recurring objects. Once I provide the details, synthesize it into a "Subconscious Dispatch Report":
1. The Manifest Symbols: Break down 2 core elements from the dream and translate them into surreal, funny, or surprisingly logical psychological mirrors (e.g., running late = your brain processing project scope creep).
2. The Shadow Narrative: What is the underlying theme or unvoiced desire this dream is attempting to highlight in your waking life?
3. The Actionable Whim: Suggest one low-stakes, highly amusing, or restorative action step I can take in the real world tomorrow to honor the dream's message.

Give me the green light to share my dream context.
```

## The three report sections

| Section | What it finds |
|---|---|
| Manifest Symbols | 2 dream elements decoded as psychological mirrors — funny, specific, and surprisingly apt |
| Shadow Narrative | The underlying waking-life theme the dream is orbiting — the thing you haven't said directly |
| Actionable Whim | One small, real-world thing to do tomorrow that acknowledges what your brain is processing |

## Jungian archetypes — accessible version

Jung's framework identifies recurring dream figures and settings as archetypes: the Shadow (the rejected self), the Anima/Animus (the inner opposite), the Trickster, the Wise Old Figure. This recipe uses those lenses playfully — not as a therapy session, but as a framework for finding the pattern underneath the surreal surface.

## What makes a good dream description

The more sensory detail, the better the report:
- Colors and light quality (dim yellow vs. bright white changes the reading)
- Emotions during the dream, not just events
- Recurring objects or figures — especially strange or out-of-place ones
- The feeling on waking — relief, dread, confusion, amusement

## The Actionable Whim — deliberately low-stakes

The third section is intentionally playful, not prescriptive. "Your brain is processing creative stagnation — tomorrow, make something small and useless just for the pleasure of making it" is more useful than a 5-step plan. The whim honors the signal without turning a dream into a project.
