**Willpower doesn't break habits — friction and replacement do.** This recipe applies behavioral science to the specific habit you want to eliminate: making the cue invisible, the execution difficult, and the reward unsatisfying — while giving the underlying craving somewhere productive to go.

## The Recipe

```
Act as a behavioral psychologist specializing in addiction, neuroplasticity, and the habit loop. I want to systematically deconstruct and eliminate an unwanted, automated habit that is draining my time, focus, or energy.

The bad habit I want to break is: [INSERT HABIT HERE]
The environment or emotional state that usually triggers it: [e.g., stress at work, boredom late at night, social media scrolling while waking up]

Please walk me through a multi-layered behavioral eradication protocol:
1. Inverting the Habit Loop: Break down how to make the environmental cue invisible, the internal craving unattractive, the physical execution incredibly difficult, and the immediate consequence unsatisfying.
2. Competing Response Design: Help me design a high-leverage "Replacement Behavior" that satisfies the same underlying neurological reward/boredom relief but is fundamentally healthy or neutral.
3. The Urge-Surfing Script: Provide a 3-minute mental script or mindfulness protocol I can deploy at the exact moment the automated craving hits to let the wave pass without giving in.
```

## The four-layer inversion

| Habit loop element | Break it by… |
|---|---|
| Cue (invisible) | Remove the trigger from your environment — phone in another room, app deleted, snack not in the house |
| Craving (unattractive) | Reframe the habit's identity — "I'm someone who doesn't do this" |
| Execution (difficult) | Add friction — 20-second delay, physical barrier, accountability partner |
| Reward (unsatisfying) | Track the consequence immediately after each slip — costs, time lost, how you felt |

## Why the replacement behavior matters

You can't just stop a habit — the underlying need (relief, stimulation, comfort) still exists. A replacement behavior that satisfies the same neurological reward reduces the craving's urgency without requiring you to white-knuckle through it.
