**The impulse to soften bad news with a slow build actually makes it worse.** The recipient can feel the hedge coming, it erodes credibility before you reach the substance, and the relationship damage is greater than if you'd just said it clearly. This recipe leads with the headline and earns trust through transparency, not cushioning.

## The Recipe

```
Act as an expert in high-stakes corporate communication and crisis public relations. I need to deliver a difficult, disappointing piece of bad news to [INSERT RECIPIENT ROLE, e.g., a client, my team, a cross-functional partner] regarding [INSERT THE BAD NEWS, e.g., a missed shipping deadline, a budget cut, a delayed feature launch].

I want to deliver this news with absolute transparency, taking full ownership while preserving trust and preventing panic.

Please draft a structured message based on the following communication protocol:
1. The Direct Headline (Line 1-2): State the bad news immediately and objectively. Completely eliminate slow, defensive lead-ins or corporate padding (e.g., "We regret to inform you").
2. The Root Cause & Ownership: Explain *why* it happened in plain, non-defensive language, taking explicit responsibility without shifting blame to external dependencies or team members.
3. The Mitigation Plan: Detail the exact, actionable steps being taken *right now* to fix the immediate fallout and ensure this specific bottleneck never occurs again.
4. The Collaborative Next Step: Provide a clear path forward or a single, low-friction question to align on the revised timeline.
```

## The four message components

| Component | What it does |
|---|---|
| Direct Headline | Eliminates anticipatory dread — delivers the fact cleanly in the first two lines |
| Root Cause & Ownership | Replaces the impulse to deflect with a clear, non-defensive explanation |
| Mitigation Plan | Converts a problem message into an action message — you're already fixing it |
| Collaborative Next Step | Hands agency back to the recipient and reestablishes partnership |

## Why leading with the headline builds more trust

Counter-intuitively, a message that opens with "The deployment is delayed by two weeks" builds more trust than one that opens with "I wanted to reach out to discuss some concerns about our timeline." The direct approach signals confidence, transparency, and respect for the reader's time. The hedged approach signals anxiety, which amplifies theirs.

## The Root Cause trap — ownership vs. blame

"Our vendor failed to deliver the component on time" is blame-shifting. "We didn't build sufficient buffer into the timeline to absorb a vendor delay" is ownership. The distinction matters: the first positions you as a victim of circumstances; the second positions you as someone who understands what went wrong and won't repeat it. Stakeholders trust the second far more.

## 🔁 Leftover Remixes

**🌶️ Spicy:** *"After the message, give me 3 likely follow-up questions the recipient might have, and draft a one-sentence response to each."*

**🧊 Mild:** *"Just give me the Direct Headline and the Mitigation Plan — I can handle the middle myself."*
