**The professional networking message that reads as transactional gets ignored. The one that reads as genuinely curious gets answered.** The difference is almost entirely about specificity — referencing a specific post, a specific domain challenge, a specific shared context — versus a generic appeal to their role or company. This recipe produces three formats, each calibrated to its platform and purpose.

## The Recipe

```
Act as an elite professional relationship architect and executive recruiter. I want to reach out to an industry peer, leader, or university alumnus on LinkedIn or email to build a genuine professional relationship, but I want to avoid looking transactional, self-serving, or demanding.

The person I am targeting is a: [INSERT ROLE/COMPANY, e.g., Principal Data Engineer at a tier-one firm]
My core goal is: [e.g., informational interview, understanding their cloud migration framework, peer community building]
My shared context or bridge: [e.g., fellow alumnus, follow their technical writing, mutual interest in eBPF/GCP]

Please generate 3 platform-native outreach variations:
- The 300-Character LinkedIn Invite: An elegant, zero-friction connection request that hooks their interest based entirely on shared technical curiosity or domain focus.
- The Asynchronous Informational Request: A short, 3-paragraph email/InMail. It must praise a specific, public project or piece of insight they shared, cleanly state my context, and ask a single, low-friction question that can be answered easily over text or a 10-minute audio message.
- The Peer Community Invite: A collaborative message framing the outreach around exchanging notes on mutual industry challenges, positioning me as an equal professional peer rather than someone asking for a favor.
```

## The three outreach formats

| Format | Platform | Tone | Goal |
|---|---|---|---|
| 300-Character LinkedIn Invite | LinkedIn connection request | Specific and frictionless | Get accepted |
| Async Informational Request | InMail or email | Curious and respectful | Earn a reply |
| Peer Community Invite | Any platform | Collaborative and equal | Start a genuine exchange |

## The 300-character constraint is the creative brief

Within 300 characters you can fit one observation and one signal of shared domain. That's exactly the right scope for a connection request — not a pitch, not a story, just "I noticed we share X, I'd value connecting." Everything else comes after acceptance.

## The "asynchronous" framing for the informational request

Most informational interview requests ask for a call. Calls require calendar coordination, synchronous time, and a certain level of social commitment. An async-first request — "this could be answered in a quick voice note or a few sentences" — dramatically lowers the friction of a yes. You're making it easier to respond than to decline.

## The Peer Community Invite — the underused format

This format positions the outreach as a mutual exchange rather than a mentorship request. "I've been working through similar problems with real-time GCP pipelines — I'd be curious to compare notes on how you've handled [specific challenge]" is a peer conversation. "I'm trying to break into your field and would love your advice" is a one-directional ask. Both might be true; only one creates a relationship.
