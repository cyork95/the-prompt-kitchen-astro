**Most professional bios describe what you do. Great ones explain why anyone should care.** This recipe interviews you for the four inputs that make positioning specific — then writes three outputs you can use immediately.

## The Recipe

```
Act as an elite executive branding specialist and personal positioning coach. I want to distill my professional background, unique advantages, and personal philosophy into a definitive, crystal-clear Personal Brand Statement.

Please interview me one question at a time to extract my positioning primitives. Do not dump all the questions at once. Over our dialogue, uncover:
1. Your Core Domain & Target Audience (Who do you solve problems for?).
2. Your Unfair Advantage / Stack (What combination of technical skills, background, or life experiences makes you uniquely qualified?).
3. The Common Industry Enemy (What status quo, bad trend, or corporate methodology do you fundamentally fight against?).
4. The Ultimate Transformation (What tangible business or personal outcome do people experience after working with you or consuming your work?).

Once I have answered all four, synthesize the data into 3 distinct Personal Brand Statements:
- The Banner Version (A crisp, 1-line bio optimized for LinkedIn/Twitter headers).
- The Elevator Pitch (A short, 2-sentence conversational introduction).
- The Manifesto Version (A high-impact, 1-paragraph positioning statement for a website home page).

Let's begin. Ask me the first question about your domain and audience.
```

## The four positioning primitives

| Primitive | What it unlocks |
|---|---|
| Core Domain & Audience | Specificity — "everyone" is not an audience |
| Unfair Advantage / Stack | Differentiation — what only you have |
| Industry Enemy | Belief — what you stand against, not just for |
| Ultimate Transformation | Relevance — the outcome, not the process |

## The three output formats

| Format | Length | Use it for |
|---|---|---|
| Banner Version | 1 line | LinkedIn/X header bio |
| Elevator Pitch | 2 sentences | Introductions, "what do you do?" moments |
| Manifesto Version | 1 paragraph | Website hero section, About page |

## Why the "Industry Enemy" question matters

A brand statement without friction is forgettable. Naming what you stand against — "I fight against fluffy strategy that never reaches execution" — instantly signals who you're for and who you're not. It's the most differentiating input of the four, and the one most people skip.
