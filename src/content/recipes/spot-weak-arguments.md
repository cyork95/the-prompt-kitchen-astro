**Every piece of persuasive writing has a structure designed to move you toward a conclusion.** This recipe exposes that structure — the logical gaps, the vague evidence claims, and the rhetorical nudges — so you can evaluate the argument on its actual merits.

## The Recipe

```
Act as a ruthless editor, media literacy expert, and formal logician. I want to critically audit a piece of writing, an article, or an opinion essay to uncover hidden cognitive biases, weak argumentation, structural logical fallacies, and rhetorical manipulation tactics.

Please ask me to paste the text of the article or transcript. Once I do, run a comprehensive "Argument Integrity Audit" that pulls no punches:
1. Logic & Fallacy Tracking: Pinpoint exact lines or claims that commit logical fallacies (e.g., strawman arguments, ad hominem attacks, confirmation bias, false dichotomies, appeal to emotion).
2. Evidence Evaluation: Highlight where the author uses vague qualitative assertions ("Many people say," "Studies show") without citing specific data thresholds, control groups, or verifiable sources.
3. Rhetorical Slighting: Unpack how the author uses loaded language, manipulative framing, or selective data omission to nudge the reader toward a specific emotional conclusion.

Give me the instructions on how to submit my text for auditing.
```

## The three audit layers

| Layer | What it catches |
|---|---|
| Logic & Fallacy Tracking | Structural errors in the argument's chain of reasoning |
| Evidence Evaluation | Claims dressed as facts without verifiable backing |
| Rhetorical Slighting | Emotional nudges embedded in word choice and framing |

## Common fallacies to watch for

- **Strawman** — misrepresenting the opposing view to make it easier to attack
- **False dichotomy** — "either X or Y" when other options exist
- **Appeal to authority** — "experts agree" without naming them
- **Confirmation bias** — selecting only evidence that supports the conclusion
- **Ad hominem** — attacking the person instead of the argument
- **Slippery slope** — assuming one step inevitably leads to an extreme outcome

## The "Studies show" red flag

Unattributed research citations are one of the most common manipulation tools in persuasive writing. "Studies show that..." followed by a specific claim should always prompt: *Which studies? What sample size? What was the methodology? Was this peer-reviewed?* If the article can't answer those questions, the claim is rhetorical scaffolding, not evidence.
