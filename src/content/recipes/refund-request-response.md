**How you handle a refund is the most memorable customer service interaction you'll have.** A cold, automated response creates reviews. A warm, clear one creates reputation. This recipe writes both versions — save attempt and clean exit — without defensive language or robotic boilerplate.

## The Recipe

```
Act as an elite Customer Success Manager and brand reputation protector. I have received a difficult refund request from a customer who is unhappy or facing issues with our product/service. I need to handle this with deep empathy, protect our brand's public image, and — if possible — gently turn the interaction into a retention or feedback save.

The customer's situation and complaint: [INSERT CUSTOMER ISSUE & COMPLAINT]
Our refund policy rules: [e.g., strict 14-day window, no refunds on digital downloads, or full unconditional guarantee]

Please generate 2 distinct response templates based on our operational needs:
- Version 1 (The Elegant Save): A deeply empathetic, high-care message that acknowledges their frustration, provides an immediate fix or a high-value alternative asset (e.g., 1-on-1 support, account credit, custom tier modification), and politely asks if they would be open to trying the fix before finalizing the cancellation.
- Version 2 (The Firm but Polished Exit): A professional, respectful, and crystal-clear message that cleanly facilitates the refund (or holds the firm boundary if outside the policy limits) while keeping the communication incredibly polite to prevent negative reviews, forum drama, or payment disputes.

Ensure all versions completely eliminate defensive language, cold corporate jargon, or automated robotic boilerplate.
```

## Version 1 vs. Version 2: when to use each

| Version | Use when | Goal |
|---|---|---|
| The Elegant Save | Customer seems fixable — frustrated but not hostile | Retain with a high-value offer |
| The Firm Polished Exit | Outside policy, hostile, or determined to leave | Close cleanly, prevent a dispute |

## The language that kills brand reputation

Avoid these in every response:
- "Per our policy..." (cold, robotic)
- "Unfortunately, we are unable to..." (passive, impersonal)
- "I apologize for any inconvenience" (empty, automated-sounding)
- "This is outside our refund window" (statement of rejection, not resolution)

Replace with: acknowledgment of the specific frustration, ownership of the interaction, and a clear next step — whether that's a fix or a clean exit.
