**Partnership pitches fail when they lead with what you want.** The ones that get replies lead with what the other side gets — their audience growth, their user value, their revenue share. This recipe flips the framing.

## The Recipe

```
Act as a strategic alliance director and business development executive. I want to pitch a mutually beneficial strategic partnership to [INSERT TARGET BRAND/INFLUENCER/COMPANY] where we combine our strengths to expand our reach, add immense value to their existing audience, and drive revenue.

My product/service value: [INSERT WHAT YOU BRING]
The target partner's audience/niche: [INSERT TARGET DETAILS]
The proposed partnership idea: [e.g., co-marketing webinar, product integration, exclusive bundle]

Draft a highly professional, low-friction outreach sequence featuring:
- The Warm Cold Outreach: A short, compelling 3-paragraph pitch message. It must lead entirely with *their* incentives, cleanly explain the synergy of the partnership, and state the explicit mutual win. Banish all vague "let's jump on a call to brainstorm" fluff.
- The 1-Page Partnership Structure Outline: A scannable framework to attach or paste below the message that details the operational logistics, revenue split models, and marketing distribution responsibilities.
- The Low-Stakes CTA: A frictionless closing question designed to simply gauge their high-level interest in the concept rather than forcing an immediate commitment.
```

## The 3-paragraph structure

| Paragraph | Content | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Their audience, their problem, their opportunity | Zero mention of you |
| 2 | The partnership concept and synergy | One sentence on your role |
| 3 | The mutual win + low-stakes ask | No "pick a time on my calendar" |

## The closing question that works

"Does this feel directionally interesting to you?" is better than "Can we jump on a 30-minute call?" One asks for an opinion. The other asks for time. Ask for opinions first — calls come after they're interested.
